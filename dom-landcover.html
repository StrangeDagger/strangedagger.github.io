Analysis of Dominant Land Cover and Sea Level Rise Impacts in the San Francisco Bay Area


In this lab, I worked on analyzing the dominant land cover types for several counties in the San Francisco Bay Area using the National Land Cover Database (NLCD) and assessed the potential impacts of sea level rise (SLR) on these land cover types. The NLCD_UTM raster dataset provided the land cover classifications, while the SLR raster layers allowed for an analysis of predicted water level changes under different sea level rise scenarios.

Key Steps:

  • Dominant Land Cover Analysis: I identified the dominant land cover for each county:
    • Sonoma County: Evergreen Forest
    • Napa County: Shrub/Scrub
    • Marin, San Francisco, and San Mateo Counties: Open Water
    • Solano, Contra Costa, and Alameda Counties: Grassland/Herbaceous
    • Santa Clara County: Mixed Forest
  • Sea Level Rise Analysis: I used two SLR layers representing current and predicted water levels (0.0m and 1.41m) and employed the Raster Calculator to calculate the difference between the two layers. This allowed me to visualize areas affected by a 1.41-meter rise in sea levels.
  • Reclassifying Land Cover: Using the Reclassify Tool, I simplified the land cover dataset to highlight the most vulnerable land cover types to rising sea levels. My analysis revealed that Emergent Herbaceous Wetlands and Open Water are the most affected, with the greatest loss occurring in these areas under the 1.41-meter SLR scenario.

Results:

The total area affected by sea level rise was approximately 465,114.70 square kilometers. The analysis showed that wetlands are particularly vulnerable to rising water levels, making them critical areas for conservation and mitigation efforts in future planning.

This lab provided experience in integrating raster data to analyze both land cover and environmental risk factors like sea level rise, enhancing my ability to perform spatial analysis using the Reclassify Tool, Raster Calculator, and land cover classification.

Tools:
